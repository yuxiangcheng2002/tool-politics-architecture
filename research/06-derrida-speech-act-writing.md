# Chapter 06: Derrida, Austin, and the Prompt as Iterable Performative
## Speech Acts, Writing, and the Architecture of the Command

> "A written sign carries with it a force of breaking with its context, that is, the set of presences which organize the moment of its inscription. This force of rupture is not an accidental predicate but the very structure of the written."
> — Jacques Derrida, "Signature Event Context," *Margins of Philosophy* (1972/1982)

> "What are we to say about this 'communication'? What is it 'communicating'? — It seems to communicate something, but what?"
> — J.L. Austin, *How to Do Things with Words* (1962)

---

## 1. Introduction

When an architect types "design a community library in the style of Tadao Ando, brutalist concrete, natural light, civic presence, photorealistic, 8k" into Midjourney, several remarkable things happen at once. The text is a description (of a desired visual output). It is also a command (to the machine: do this). It is a kind of design brief (summarizing architectural intent). It is, possibly, a claim of authorship (the architect calls this "their design"). And it is addressed to a system that has no capacity for understanding any of it — only for statistical pattern-matching.

This chapter reads the architectural AI prompt through two theoretical lenses: J.L. Austin's speech act theory — particularly his concept of the *performative* utterance — and Derrida's deconstruction of Austin in "Signature Event Context" (Margins of Philosophy, 1972). Together, they allow a precise analysis of the prompt's strange ontological status: it is a performative that works (it executes, it produces an effect) without a subject who intends it, without a context that authorizes it, and without an addressee who understands it.

The prompt is, in Derrida's terms, an *iterable* mark — a piece of writing that retains its functional force across all contexts, in the absence of any originating intention. And this iterability, which Derrida saw as the deconstructive heart of writing, is precisely what makes AI prompt-response systems possible.

## 2. Austin's Speech Act Theory: Performatives and Constatives

### 2.1 How to Do Things with Words

Austin's foundational distinction (developed in his 1955 Harvard lectures, published as *How to Do Things with Words*, 1962): between *constative* and *performative* utterances.

**Constative**: A statement that describes a state of affairs and can be evaluated as true or false. "The building has twelve floors." "This is a brutalist structure."

**Performative**: An utterance that, in saying something, *does* something — it constitutes or transforms a state of affairs. It cannot be true or false; it can succeed (*felicitous*) or fail (*infelicitous*).

Classic examples:
- "I hereby declare this building open." (said by an official at a ribbon-cutting)
- "I promise to deliver the drawings by Friday."
- "I name this ship the *Enterprise*."

These utterances are not reports of actions; they *are* the actions. The declaration *is* the opening; the promise *is* the commitment; the naming *is* the christening.

### 2.2 Conditions of Felicity

Austin enumerates the conditions for a performative to succeed — to be *felicitous*. These include:

1. **Conventional procedure**: There must be an accepted social procedure into which the utterance fits ("I hereby open this building" only works at an opening ceremony)
2. **Correct persons and circumstances**: The right people must speak in the right circumstances ("I pronounce you married" only works if said by an authorized official)
3. **Correct execution**: The procedure must be executed fully and correctly
4. **Sincere intention**: The speaker must have the relevant intentions

If these conditions are not met, the performative fails — not because it says something false but because it doesn't *do* what it claims to do. Austin calls this an "infelicity."

### 2.3 The Illocutionary Act

Austin later refined his analysis with the tripartite distinction:

- **Locutionary act**: The act of producing meaningful sound/text — what is *said*
- **Illocutionary act**: The act *performed in* saying — the force (promising, warning, declaring, commanding)
- **Perlocutionary act**: The effect *produced by* saying — persuading, alarming, starting, convincing

The illocutionary force is what differentiates "I warn you" from "I inform you" from "I command you" — same locution, different illocutionary act, potentially different perlocutionary effects.

## 3. The Architectural Prompt as Speech Act

### 3.1 What Kind of Speech Act Is a Prompt?

The architectural AI prompt is a strange speech act. Consider:

> "a brutalist concrete library, civic monumentality, natural skylights, dramatic shadow, Tadao Ando, photorealistic, cinematic, 8k"

What is the *illocutionary force* of this utterance?

- **Descriptive?** It lists properties, but it is not asserting that any building with these properties exists.
- **Imperative?** It has the grammar of a list, not a command, but it functions to produce an action (the image generation).
- **Constitutive?** It partly *constitutes* the image it describes — the prompt is not describing a pre-existing image but calling it into existence.
- **Design brief?** It functions like an architect's brief to a renderer — specifying what is wanted.
- **Ontological declaration?** It specifies *what kind of thing* should exist (the type, the genre, the aesthetic).

The prompt collapses Austin's distinctions. Its locutionary form is descriptive (list of properties); its illocutionary force is imperative (make this exist) and constitutive (this is what the image should be); its perlocutionary effect is the produced image.

### 3.2 The Felicity Conditions of the Prompt

By Austin's criteria, what conditions must hold for a prompt to succeed?

1. **Conventional procedure**: There must be a functioning AI system capable of responding to text prompts (Midjourney, SD, DALL-E). ✓ when system is working
2. **Correct persons and circumstances**: A subscribed user typing a valid prompt. ✓ (partially — subscription required)
3. **Correct execution**: The prompt must be syntactically valid (not too long, not banned content). ✓ if within limits
4. **Sincere intention**: The user must *intend* to generate an image. Here the analysis gets interesting...

**The sincerity condition** is the most revealing. Austin required that performatives be uttered with sincere intention — the promise must be genuinely intended, the warning must reflect genuine concern. But the AI system doesn't require or verify sincerity. A prompt can be typed as a test, as a joke, by accident, by a script, by another AI — and the image is generated identically regardless of the "speaker's" intention.

This suggests that the prompt satisfies all of Austin's *external* conditions for performative success but makes the *internal* condition (sincerity, intention) irrelevant. The performative works without a sincere subject behind it.

## 4. Derrida's Deconstruction: Iterability and the Absence of the Subject

### 4.1 "Signature Event Context"

Derrida's 1972 essay "Signature Event Context" (published in *Margins of Philosophy*) is a reading — and deconstruction — of Austin's performative. Derrida's central argument: Austin's analysis of the performative is undermined by an inevitable feature of all writing that Austin tried to exclude as "abnormal" or "parasitic."

Austin had acknowledged that performatives can fail in theatrical or fictional contexts: an actor who says "I promise..." on stage is not really promising; a novelist who writes "I hereby declare..." is not really declaring. Austin called these "parasitic" uses — dependent on the normal, sincere use for their meaning.

Derrida's deconstruction: these supposedly "parasitic" uses are not exceptions to the normal functioning of the performative. They are *constitutive* of it. The performative works *because* it is iterable — because it can be cited, repeated, quoted, grafted into new contexts, even in the absence of an original sincere utterer. This iterability is not a defect; it is the condition of possibility of the performative's force.

> "A writing that is not structurally readable — iterable — beyond the death of the given addressee would not be writing."

### 4.2 The Iterable Sign

Derrida's key concept: **iterability** (from *iter*, again, + *itara*, the Sanskrit for other). Every sign — every word, every letter, every gesture, every code — is iterable: it can be repeated, in different contexts, by different speakers, for different purposes, producing different effects. This repeatability-in-difference is not incidental to the sign but structural.

For a sign to be a sign at all, it must be recognizable across different contexts — which means it must be able to function in the *absence* of its original context, its original speaker, its original intention. A word that could only be used once, by one person, in one context, would not be a word.

**Consequence for performatives**: The performative works by virtue of its iterability — the declaration "I hereby open this building" works because it cites a conventional formula that has been used, recognized, and repeated across many similar contexts. Its force is not the product of this particular speaker's intention but of the iterable formula's conventional authority.

The "sincere" original intention that Austin requires is always already haunted by the citational structure that makes the utterance possible in the first place.

### 4.3 The Death of the Addressee (and the Author)

Derrida pushes further: writing, he argues, is constituted by the structural possibility of functioning in the absence of its addressee — even, ultimately, in the absence of its author.

> "A written sign carries with it a force of breaking with its context... the mark, and in particular the written mark, carries the force of breaking with its context."

This is not a special feature of written language as opposed to spoken; it is a feature of *all* signs. But writing makes it structurally explicit: a letter can be read after the sender has died; a law can be enforced after all its drafters are gone; an architectural drawing can be built from after its architect has passed.

**Applied to the AI prompt**: The architectural prompt is the purest instance of this condition. The prompt "a brutalist concrete library" generates the same (approximately) image whether:
- Typed by the architect who designed the building program
- Copied from a tutorial
- Generated by another AI as a test prompt
- Submitted by a student who has never heard of Tadao Ando
- Posted by a bot as part of a spam campaign

The "author" of the prompt is structurally irrelevant to its execution. The AI system receives the text-as-text, not the intention behind it. This is not a bug; it is the structural condition of all writing taken to its logical conclusion.

## 5. The Prompt Between Natural Language and Code

### 5.1 Austin's Exclusion of Writing

Derrida noted that Austin's speech act theory was, despite its title, oriented toward *speech* — toward the full presence of a speaker in a context, with intentions visible (in principle) to an interlocutor. Writing introduced complications Austin tried to handle by relegating them to "abnormal" cases.

The AI prompt is neither speech nor ordinary writing. It is:
- **Written** (typed text, addressed to a machine)
- **But speech-like** (imperative, immediate, constitutive)
- **Addressed** to a system that cannot understand it
- **Executed** by a process that produces effects without comprehending them

This is the "new ambiguous hybrid" in Yuxiang's framework: the prompt has the form of writing but the force of a command, addressed to a non-subject that responds as if to a subject.

### 5.2 Kittler's Executable Code vs. Derrida's Iterable Sign

Kittler argued that code is writing that executes — that the distinction between description and action collapses in the program. A program is not a description of operations; it *is* the operations (when run on appropriate hardware).

Derrida would say: this is not unique to code; it is what writing has always been, once we abandon the metaphysics of presence. Writing always carries a force that exceeds its author's intention, that can be "executed" — taken up, put to work — by contexts its author never intended.

The prompt sits exactly at the intersection of these two analyses:
- It is iterable (Derrida): its force is independent of the author's intention
- It is executable (Kittler): it produces effects in the material world via hardware processes

The "execution" of the prompt is both semiotic (Derrida — it cites conventions of visual description, design brief, aesthetic specification) and material (Kittler — it triggers GPU processes via API call).

### 5.3 Austin's Three Acts Collapsed

Austin's tripartite distinction — locutionary, illocutionary, perlocutionary — is methodologically collapsed in the AI prompt:

- **Locutionary** (what is said): "brutalist concrete library, civic monumentality..."
- **Illocutionary** (what is done in saying): the act of specifying, commanding, constituting
- **Perlocutionary** (what is produced): the generated image

In ordinary speech acts, these are analytically distinct and can come apart (saying X doesn't always *do* Y, doing Y doesn't always *produce* Z). In the AI prompt, they are *technically fused*: the locution is *simultaneously* the illocutionary act *and* (with negligible delay) the perlocutionary effect. The gap between saying and doing — which is the space of social mediation, negotiation, and failure — is eliminated by technical execution.

This fusion is the source of the prompt's power and its politics: it produces effects with the immediacy of command and the authority of code, while appearing to have the lightness and reversibility of natural language.

## 6. The System Prompt as Ontological Declaration

### 6.1 "You Are an Architect"

The *system prompt* in AI systems is an instruction given to the model before user interaction begins — it sets the "context" for all subsequent responses. System prompts typically take the form of ontological declarations: "You are a helpful assistant." "You are an expert architect." "You are a creative visual artist with expertise in brutalist design."

These declarations are performative in Austin's strongest sense: they do not describe an existing state of affairs but *constitute* one. By saying "you are an architect," the system prompt does not report that the AI is an architect; it attempts to install a subject-position, a role, a set of dispositions that will govern subsequent outputs.

**The felicity condition**: Does the system prompt succeed? Does it actually make the AI "be" an architect? Austin would say: it depends on whether the conditions for felicity are met. For human performatives (knighting, appointing), there are social institutions that back the performative. For AI system prompts, the only "institution" is the model's training — its learned associations between "being an architect" and certain patterns of language, knowledge, and output style.

### 6.2 The Performative Without Subject

The system prompt "You are an architect" is a performative addressed to a non-subject. The AI language model has no subjectivity, no desires, no professional identity. The performative *appears* to succeed (the AI responds differently after being told it is an architect) but this is not genuine subject-constitution — it is statistical pattern activation.

Derrida would say: this is the limit case of iterability. The performative formula "You are X" has always worked through iterable citation — it cites the convention of subject-constitution, the social form of role-assignment. In the AI case, the citational structure is retained but the subject is absent. The performative "works" (in the sense that it produces effects) without any subject being constituted. This is Derrida's prediction (all writing works in the absence of subjects) taken to its technical limit.

### 6.3 Architecture as Ontological Declaration

When an architect presents an AI-generated image as their "design vision," they are performing an ontological declaration: "this is what I intend to build." The image itself — generated from statistical patterns, not from the architect's design process — is being re-authored through the act of presentation.

This is *retroactive* performativity: the authorial claim constitutes the image as a design intention after the fact. The image was generated by a statistical process; the act of presenting it as design vision *makes it* design vision — or claims to.

This retroactive performativity is both aesthetically interesting (it raises genuinely productive questions about authorship and design intention) and professionally problematic (it obscures the actually generative process, concealing the AI's role from clients, collaborators, and juries).

## 7. Writing, Absence, and the Architecture of the Prompt

### 7.1 The Prompt as Architectural Writing

Derrida's essay ends with a discussion of signature — the mark that links a text to a subject, that attempts to anchor the iterable sign to a singular origin. The signature "both affirms and denies the rupture" of writing from intention: it tries to re-attach the text to its author, but it can itself be forged, iterated, cited.

Architectural drawing conventions are a form of signature: the title block with architect's name and seal, the copyright notice on drawings, the firm stamp on construction documents. These are signatures that attempt to fix the iterable architectural drawing to a responsible subject.

AI-generated architectural images lack this signature structure — or rather, they have *platform* signatures (Midjourney watermarks, DALL-E generation metadata) but no architect's signature. The attempt to add an architect's signature (presenting AI images as one's own work) is precisely the grafting of a signature onto an iterable text that structurally resists any singular origin.

### 7.2 The Loss of Presence in Architectural Communication

Derrida's analysis of the "absence" structuring all writing is relevant to a specific change in architectural communication:

Traditional architectural communication involved degrees of presence:
- The architect's hand in the drawing (presence through craft)
- The architect's voice in the client presentation (full presence)
- The architect's signature on construction documents (legal presence)
- The physical model in the meeting room (material presence)

AI-generated image communication involves a different structure of presence/absence:
- No architect's hand (generated statistically)
- Architect's voice in presentation (but presenting others' work)
- No craft signature (just subscription to a platform)
- No material presence (images only, no spatial simulation)

The fundamental absence at the heart of AI architectural representation — the absence of an authoring hand, an originating decision, a disciplinary judgment — is exactly what Derrida's analysis of writing prepares us to see.

## 8. Application to Architecture Assignment

### 8.1 The Prompt as Critical Object

A critical architectural paper on AI image generation should treat the prompt as its primary object of analysis — not just as a technical input but as a speech act with specific structure, politics, and implications.

Analysis questions:
1. **Locutionary**: What keywords does the architect use? What aesthetic vocabulary is available vs. unavailable in prompt language?
2. **Illocutionary**: What is the architect *doing* in prompting? Commissioning? Specifying? Imagining? Performing authorship?
3. **Perlocutionary**: What effect does the prompt have — on the image, on the architect's imagination, on the client's expectations?

### 8.2 The Iterability Problem in Architectural Practice

Derrida's iterability raises a practical problem for architectural authorship: if prompt-based image generation works through iterable formulas (standard aesthetic descriptors that anyone can use), then the same prompt generates similar images regardless of who prompts. Architectural differentiation — the firm's "signature style," the architect's "design vision" — cannot be secured at the prompt level.

This is verifiable: take any high-profile architectural firm's aesthetic descriptor ("parametric, fluid, organic, glass, steel, BIG-style, drone view") and submit it to Midjourney — the output is nearly identical regardless of who submits it. The "design vision" is a formula, and formulas are iterable.

**Implication**: Professional architectural authority cannot be grounded in AI image generation, because the tool produces iterable outputs from iterable inputs. Authority must be grounded elsewhere — in the non-iterable: the specific site, the specific program, the specific client relationship, the specific structural solution, the specific material knowledge. These are the registers that AI image generation cannot access.

### 8.3 Comparison Table: Speech Act Analysis of Architectural Communication

| Mode | Locution | Illocutionary Force | Perlocutionary Effect | Subject? |
|------|----------|--------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| Design brief | Prose description | Commissioning, specifying | Renders produced | Yes (client + architect) |
| Architectural drawing | Graphic notation | Constituting (making it) | Building constructed | Yes (architect + contractor) |
| Physical model | Spatial object | Showing, proposing | Client imagines | Yes (architect's hand) |
| 3D render brief to artist | Text + sketches | Commissioning | Render produced | Yes (architect + renderer) |
| AI prompt | Text string | Commanding statistical process | Image generated | No stable subject |

The critical column: **Subject?** Only in the AI prompt case is the subject absent or unstable. The prompt functions as a performative without a subject — it executes effects without a person who intends, understands, or is responsible for those effects.

## 9. Summary

Austin's speech act theory and Derrida's deconstruction of it provide the most precise theoretical apparatus for understanding the architectural AI prompt's strange status. The prompt is a performative that works without Austin's conditions of felicity — without sincere intention, without authorizing social institution, without an addressee who understands. It is an iterable mark whose force is independent of any originating subject.

This has profound implications for architectural authorship and responsibility. The prompt-as-iterable-performative produces the image, but the "author" is absent — replaced by a statistical process that operates on the prompt's text without understanding its architectural, spatial, or cultural content. The retroactive claim of authorship (presenting AI images as "design vision") is an attempt to anchor an iterable text to a singular origin — precisely the move Derrida shows to be always already compromised.

For architectural practice and education, this analysis clarifies what is at stake in AI image generation: not just a faster rendering tool but a transformation of the structure of architectural communication, authorship, and responsibility. The discipline's response must engage this transformation at the level of theory and practice — not simply adopting or refusing the tool, but rethinking what architectural authorship means in a world of iterable, itinerant, subject-free image generation.
